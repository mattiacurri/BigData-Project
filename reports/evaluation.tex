\paragraph{Overall Performance Comparison.} The \textcolor{drawred}{\textbf{Original}} implementation achieves significantly higher MAP (0.87 vs. 0.78) and Macro F1 (0.86 vs. 0.69), indicating superior ranking quality and classification performance. However, the \textcolor{drawblue}{\textbf{Incremental}} approach achieves notably higher AUC (0.80 vs. 0.65), suggesting better discrimination ability at various probability thresholds.

\paragraph{Choosing MAP as discriminative metric.} Choosing MAP as the primary metric for model selection is justified by its focus on ranking quality, which is critical for link prediction tasks. However, as we can see in the first configuration of the \textcolor{drawblue}{\textbf{Incremental}} approach, the best model predicts always 1, in practice the model is not able to discriminate between the two classes.

\paragraph{Negative Sampling Multiplier Impact.} For both approaches, increasing NM from 1 to 2, it reduces MAP (\textcolor{drawblue}{\textbf{Incremental}}: 0.78 $\rightarrow$ 0.64; \textcolor{drawred}{\textbf{Original}}: 0.87 $\rightarrow$ 0.77), as expected. However, it improves AUC (\textcolor{drawblue}{\textbf{Incremental}}: 0.78 $\rightarrow$ 0.72; \textcolor{drawred}{\textbf{Original}}: 0.63 $\rightarrow$ 0.65), suggesting that a higher proportion of negative samples helps the model learn better decision boundaries, albeit at the cost of ranking quality.

\paragraph{Learning Rate Impact.} The learning rate shows different effects across approaches:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textcolor{drawblue}{\textbf{Incremental}}: LR=0.0005 achieves higher AUC (0.8037) but with worse class balance
	\item \textcolor{drawred}{\textbf{Original}}: LR=0.001 consistently outperforms LR=0.0005 across all metrics
\end{itemize}

In general, the \textcolor{drawred}{\textbf{Original}} approach demonstrates stronger performance in terms of MAP and Macro F1.

\subsubsection*{Analysis of Synthetic Networks}

The synthetic network generated by the \textcolor{drawblue}{\textbf{Incremental}} configuration (LR=0.0005, NM=1) exhibits significant structural differences compared to real social networks:

\paragraph{Degree Distribution.} The synthetic network shows an extremely high average degree (220.35), approximately 7$\times$ higher than the Gab dataset (32.11) and 5$\times$ higher than Google+ (127.06). This indicates that the model tends to predict edges too liberally, creating an overly dense network.
% In real social networks, users typically maintain a limited number of connections due to cognitive constraints and social capital limitations, resulting in sparse graph structures.

\paragraph{Embedding Similarity.} It is highly probable that the synthetic posts produce very similar embeddings, which biases the model toward predicting a disproportionate number of connections among users; this embedding similarity therefore contributes directly to the generation of an excessively dense synthetic network.

\paragraph{Community Structure.} The near-zero modularity (-0.0013) indicates a complete absence of community structure,opposed to the strong modularity observed in real networks (0.27-0.83). This suggests that the model creates random-like structure where users are equally likely to connect regardless of their features.
%The synthetic network's lack of modularity suggests that the model does not capture the homophilic patterns that drive real social network formation.

\paragraph{Small-World Property.} The average path length of 1.0167 is unrealistically low compared to real networks. This is a direct consequence of the high density: in a nearly complete graph, most nodes are directly connected or separated by only one intermediate node. Real social networks maintain the ``small-world'' property with moderate path lengths while remaining sparse.

\paragraph{Clustering Coefficient.} Interestingly, the clustering coefficient (0.5941) falls within the range of real social networks (0.49-0.61). This suggests that when the model does predict edges, it tends to close triangles.

% The analysis reveals several key observations:

% \paragraph{Dimensional Collapse and Oversmoothing.} The Original implementation configurations show extreme concentration in the first principal component (95-100\%), indicating \textbf{dimensional collapse}: the GCN embeddings collapse to a nearly one-dimensional space. This phenomenon is closely related to \textbf{oversmoothing} in Graph Neural Networks, where repeated message passing operations cause node representations to become indistinguishable from one another \cite{liDeeperInsightGraph2018,oonoGraphNeuralNetworks2020}. After multiple GCN layers, each node's embedding becomes dominated by the aggregated neighborhood statistics rather than its individual features.

The \textcolor{drawred}{\textbf{Original}} implementation configurations show extreme concentration in the first principal component (95-100\%), indicating \textbf{dimensional collapse}: the GCN embeddings collapse to a nearly one-dimensional space. In contrast, the \textcolor{drawblue}{\textbf{Incremental}} implementation shows more variance distribution: the configuration with LR=0.0005, NM=1 achieves only 76.13\% on PC1, with 22.61\% on PC2, suggesting a richer embedding space. This may explain why that particular run is the only one that attempts to connect synthetic users, even if with poor performance.
